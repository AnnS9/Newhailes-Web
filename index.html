Newhailes House and Gardens


Newhailes House is a Palladian style country house in Musselburgh, East Lothian. House was built in 1686 for James Smith, who was also the architect of this place. The house is situated on 80 acres of parkland belonging to it, There are several footpaths leading around the estate with the views of the Forth, the Shell Grotto, Earl of Stair's Monument, old stables, Summer Tea House and water gardens. The property is currently owned by the National Trust for Scotland, it is possible to visit the house with a guide, there is also cafe, a play area for children and a space for picnic and community garden. House was Originally named Whitehills, and belongs to the A Buildings category, meaning: "Buildings of national or international importance, either architectural or historic, or fine little-altered examples of some particular period, style or building type." Building comprises the original 7-bay frontage flanked by later extensions.

Stables and office
Situated to the south-west of Newhailes House, the stables and office block was built as part of the changes initiated on the estate in 1798 (with the north and east ranges constructed in 1826). They comprise a two-storey quadrangular Classical stable court, incorporating late 17th century to mid-18th century builds in the north-west and south-west ranges.
Art collection on display in house
Several painting are on display within the house, largely Dalrymple family portraits by Scottish painter Allan Ramsay (artist),(1713-1784) and selection of local landscapes by James Norie (1711-1736)..
Shell Grotto
Shell Grotto was Commissioned by Lord Hailes, built c.1785, as a pleasure ground to the north of the house. There are the remains of square plan rocaille grotto set in woodlands, currently roofless. The interior formerly lined with decorative seashell patterns, mounted on timber panels, of which only remnants remain in woodland around.
